## What is the DMCA?

The Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA) is a US law that provides a process for copyright holders to request removal of infringing content from online platforms.

**Common scenarios on webcam.org:**
- Camera incidentally captures copyrighted music playing outdoors
- Camera view includes TV screen showing copyrighted content
- User uploads a video clip containing copyrighted material

**Note:** Incidental, unintentional capture of copyrighted content in the background of a live camera stream is often considered fair use, but we will respond to valid DMCA notices.

## How to Report Copyright Infringement

### Before You File

**Ask yourself:**
1. Do you own the copyright or represent the copyright holder?
2. Is the content actually infringing (not fair use, public domain, or licensed)?
3. Have you tried contacting the camera owner directly?

### What We Need (DMCA Requirements)

Your notice must include **ALL** of the following:

1. **Your Signature**
   - Physical or electronic signature of the copyright owner or authorized agent

2. **Identification of Copyrighted Work**
   - Describe the copyrighted work you claim is infringed
   - If multiple works, provide a representative list

3. **Identification of Infringing Material**
   - URL of the camera or specific content
   - Timestamp if relevant (live streams change constantly)
   - Specific description so we can locate it

4. **Your Contact Information**
   - Name, address, phone number, email

5. **Good Faith Statement**
   - Statement that you have a good faith belief that the use is not authorized by the copyright owner, its agent, or the law

6. **Accuracy Statement**
   - Statement under penalty of perjury that the information is accurate and you are authorized to act on behalf of the copyright owner

### Where to Send

**Email:** dmca@webcam.org (preferred)
**Mail:**
```
DMCA Agent
webcam.org
[ADDRESS TO BE ADDED]
```

**Response time:** We aim to respond within 24-48 hours.

## Our DMCA Process

### Step 1: Review (1-2 business days)
- We verify the notice meets DMCA requirements
- We assess whether the claim appears valid
- We may request clarification if notice is incomplete

### Step 2: Action (24-48 hours)
**If notice is valid:**
- We disable access to or remove the allegedly infringing content
- We notify the camera owner of the takedown
- We provide the camera owner with your DMCA notice (minus personal info if requested)

**If notice is invalid:**
- We notify you of deficiencies
- You may resubmit a corrected notice

### Step 3: Counter-Notice Window (10-14 business days)
- Camera owner has the right to file a counter-notice
- If no counter-notice is received, content stays down
- If counter-notice is received, we forward it to you

### Step 4: Resolution (10-14 business days after counter-notice)
- You may file a lawsuit to keep content down
- If no lawsuit is filed, we may restore content
- If lawsuit is filed, content stays down pending court decision

## Counter-Notices (For Camera Owners)

### If Your Camera Was Taken Down

If you believe the DMCA notice was:
- Mistaken (not actually infringing)
- Fair use (incidental capture, transformative, etc.)
- Public domain or licensed
- Otherwise invalid

**You may file a counter-notice.**

### What We Need (Counter-Notice Requirements)

Your counter-notice must include:

1. **Your Signature**
   - Physical or electronic signature

2. **Identification of Removed Material**
   - Description of the material and its location before removal

3. **Good Faith Statement**
   - Statement under penalty of perjury that you have a good faith belief the material was removed due to mistake or misidentification

4. **Consent to Jurisdiction**
   - Statement that you consent to the jurisdiction of Federal District Court in [JURISDICTION] or, if outside the US, any judicial district in which webcam.org may be found
   - Statement that you will accept service of process from the person who filed the DMCA notice

5. **Your Contact Information**
   - Name, address, phone number, email

### Where to Send

**Email:** dmca@webcam.org
**Subject:** DMCA Counter-Notice

## Repeat Infringer Policy

### Three Strikes

We take copyright infringement seriously and have a **repeat infringer policy**:

**First violation:**
- Content removed
- Warning issued
- Account remains active

**Second violation:**
- Content removed
- Account suspended for 30 days
- Public camera privileges revoked

**Third violation:**
- Account terminated permanently
- All content removed
- IP may be blocked

**Exceptions:**
- Clear fair use cases don't count as strikes
- Mistakes (incomplete notices) don't count
- Successful counter-notices remove strikes

## Fair Use Considerations

### When DMCA May Not Apply

**Fair use** is a legal defense to copyright infringement. Factors include:
1. Purpose (commercial vs. educational)
2. Nature of copyrighted work
3. Amount used
4. Effect on market value

**Examples that may be fair use:**
- Incidental music playing in background of outdoor camera
- Brief snippets of copyrighted content in larger context
- Transformative use (commentary, criticism, parody)
- Non-commercial, informational sharing

**Note:** Fair use is complex. We are not judges and cannot make fair use determinations. If you believe your use is fair use, file a counter-notice.

## Special Situations

### Live Streams
Copyright claims on live camera streams are complicated:
- Content is constantly changing
- Infringement may be brief and unintentional
- Camera owner may not even be aware

**Our approach:**
- We'll notify camera owner immediately
- Camera owner can adjust angle or mute audio
- Persistent, intentional infringement = takedown

### Incidental Background Content
Cameras may incidentally capture:
- Music from passing cars
- TV through neighbor's window
- Radio from outdoor speaker

**This is often considered de minimis (too minor) or fair use.**

We assess each claim individually based on:
- Duration and prominence of copyrighted content
- Intent of camera owner
- Nature of the use

### Public Performances
Some copyrighted content in public spaces may be:
- Licensed for public performance
- Fair use due to transformative nature
- De minimis (too minor to infringe)

**Example:** Street musician performing copyrighted songs is likely licensed or fair use.

## False DMCA Notices

### Penalties for Misrepresentation

Under 17 U.S.C. ยง 512(f), anyone who knowingly misrepresents that:
- Material is infringing, or
- Material was removed by mistake

**May be liable for damages**, including:
- Actual damages (lost revenue, legal fees)
- Attorney's fees
- Court costs

**We may pursue legal action against repeat false notices.**
